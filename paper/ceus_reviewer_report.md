# Reviewer Report

**Manuscript:** Opportunistic License Plate Recognition from Urban Imaging Sensors: Quantifying AI-Enabled Recoverability Under Uncontrolled Viewing Angles
**Journal:** Computers, Environment and Urban Systems (CEUS)
**Recommendation:** Major Revision

---

## 1. Summary of the paper

The authors build a fully synthetic benchmark of Israeli-format license plates rendered at 256x64 pixels, distort them across the full angular grid [0, 89] x [0, 89] degrees using Scrambled Sobol sampling plus a fixed degradation pipeline (Gaussian blur, JPEG 55-85, colour jitter, edge blending), and train five paired image-to-image restoration networks: U-Net, an angle-conditioned U-Net with FiLM, Restormer, Pix2Pix GAN, and a velocity-prediction SR3 diffusion model. Each of the 10 model x dataset pairs is evaluated on 8,100 angle cells using plate-level PSNR, SSIM, and Tesseract OCR accuracy. The authors introduce two summary metrics, "boundary-AUC" (fraction of the angle grid where OCR exceeds a threshold T = 0.9) and a reliability score F (RMS distance of interior failure points from the boundary). Headline claims: (i) up to 93.4% of the angular grid is "recoverable" by the union of all models, (ii) discriminative architectures dominate generative ones, (iii) lateral (alpha) rotation is harder than elevational (beta), (iv) PSNR tracks OCR tightly (R^2 = 0.99) whereas SSIM is threshold-like and unreliable. The paper is framed as an "opportunistic sensing" contribution for smart city infrastructure.

## 2. Fit for CEUS

Marginal. CEUS publishes work where computation is in service of environmental or urban systems questions; it is not an outlet for pure computer-vision benchmarks. The authors have clearly made an effort to reframe what is, at heart, a restoration benchmark into an "urban sensor repurposing" narrative, invoking ATM cameras, body-worn devices, dashcams, and pole-mounted CCTV, and citing Batty, Kitchin, Goodchild, Zheng, and Townsend. However the framing is thin: none of the putative sensor classes are actually modelled. There is no real urban geometry, no sensor catalogue, no deployment data, no discussion of pixel-on-plate as a function of focal length and vehicle distance, and no engagement with the urban-planning, legal, or ethical dimensions of opportunistic vehicle identity extraction (which are substantial for a CEUS readership). The "smart city" material in Section 2.1 and Section 6.1 reads as bolted-on. The angular boundary result is interesting, but as written the paper could equally well appear in a computer-vision venue, and a CEUS reader will find little that is specifically urban-systems in scope. I would lean toward recommending transfer to a CV or ITS journal unless the authors can substantially deepen the urban-systems contribution (see required revisions).

## 3. Strengths

- The research question, "at which viewing angles does restoration-enabled LPR still succeed?", is concrete and answerable, and the paper does answer it.
- The full-grid evaluation (8,100 angle pairs, with 10-sample density in the high-distortion region) is more systematic than typical LPR benchmarks, which sample a few canonical angles.
- The Sobol-based sampling design is well justified (Section 2.4) and appropriate.
- Restoration architectures cover a sensible spread across discriminative and generative families.
- The PSNR-OCR versus SSIM-OCR comparison (Fig. 6, Fig. 7, Eqs. 10-13) is a genuinely useful practical finding: the threshold behaviour of SSIM and its high residual variance at fixed SSIM is important for practitioners selecting training losses.
- The honest admission in the Conclusion and Section 6.4 that the study is synthetic-only is welcome.
- Writing is clear and the figures (after the recent revision cutting the redundant heatmap/boundary schematic pair) are better paced than most restoration papers.

## 4. Weaknesses and concerns (ranked by severity)

1. **Synthetic-only evaluation with no real-world validation.** Every conclusion, including the 93.4% headline, depends on a single font (Israeli plate), a single resolution (256x64), a constant camera-to-plate distance, and a narrow hand-tuned degradation model (sigma in [0.5, 1.5], JPEG quality 55-85). Real opportunistic captures from ATM, dashcam, and body-worn cameras have scale variation spanning roughly an order of magnitude in pixels-per-character, motion blur, rolling-shutter skew, severe low-light noise, multi-font plates, dirt, partial occlusion, and nonlinear optical distortion. There is no evidence that the numerical boundary generalises. The manuscript acknowledges this in Section 6.4 but continues to state the 93.4% figure in the Abstract, Contributions, Conclusion, and Discussion as if it were a deployment guarantee. This is the single largest issue.

2. **Internal inconsistency between the paper and the experiment data.** The paper (Table 1) reports two datasets (Standard DS-S and Extreme DS-E, 10,240 pairs each). The supporting `experiment_data.md` consistently reports three datasets (A, B, C), with B containing 20,480 samples, and it further attributes claims such as "AUC gain from doubling data (A to B) < 0.01" that are discussed in neither the paper nor its Table. Either the paper silently dropped a dataset during revision without updating derivative claims, or the experiment record was never reconciled. The Section 6.3 sentence "A dataset with 10,000 well-distributed angle pairs will generalise at least as well as one with 20,000 concentrated near moderate angles" is presented as a finding yet no 20,000-sample dataset is reported in the paper text. This must be fixed before acceptance.

3. **Novelty of "boundary-AUC" and F is overstated.** Equation 5 integrates the upper envelope of a 2-D binary map and divides by grid area; this is a straightforward 2-D generalisation of the classical survival curve integral / ROC-style AUC, and readers will recognise it as such. Calling it a "novel metric" twice in the Contributions list is too strong. F (Eq. 7) is simply an RMS boundary-distance of interior failures; this is closer to an erosion/hole-penetration measure that exists in the mathematical-morphology literature. The authors should either cite prior work on 2-D recoverability envelopes (e.g., operating curves for recognition under compound degradation) and position these metrics as adaptations, or rename them so the naming does not overpromise.

4. **No statistical uncertainty.** Every number in Tables 3 and 4 is a point estimate from a single training run. No seeds, no confidence intervals, no significance tests. Differences like Restormer AUC 0.920 versus U-Net 0.915 on DS-S, or F 0.095 versus 0.103, are within the noise one typically sees from reshuffled initialisation. With 10 model-dataset pairs and claims ranked on margins of 0.5%, at minimum 3 seeds per cell and bootstrap CIs on AUC and F are required to make the rank orderings defensible.

5. **"Opportunistic sensing" framing is largely rhetorical.** The paper repeatedly invokes ATMs, body-worn cameras, dashcams, and streetlight CCTV in the Introduction, Section 6.1, and Fig. 1, yet none of these sensor classes is characterised or modelled. There is no mapping from sensor type to typical (alpha, beta) distribution, no pixels-on-plate budget, no deployment height / focal-length survey. The "camera placement guideline" in Section 6.1 (prioritise azimuthal angle over elevational angle) is not derived from any urban-sensor data; it follows purely from the synthetic asymmetry. For a CEUS audience this link from the benchmark result to actual urban sensor planning is the contribution that justifies publication, and it is currently absent.

6. **Reproducibility.** No code URL, no data release, no model weights, no seeds. Tesseract v4 LSTM with multi-strategy fallback is described in prose, not scripted. An independent reader cannot reproduce a single number in Tables 3 or 4. For a benchmark paper this is disqualifying in current form.

7. **OCR choice is load-bearing but under-justified.** Tesseract v4 is an uncommonly weak baseline for LPR in 2024-2026; modern specialised plate OCR (e.g., PaddleOCR, CRNN trained on synthetic plates) would give substantially different threshold behaviour. The "93.4%" number is specifically 93.4% relative to Tesseract's preprocessing chain, not an intrinsic recoverability limit. The paper should either use a contemporary plate-OCR model or report sensitivity of all headline numbers to OCR choice.

8. **Related-work gaps.** The Oblique-LPR literature (super-resolution LPR, e.g., Lu et al.'s perspective-corrected LPR, work on low-resolution LPR benchmarks such as UFPR-ALPR and RodoSol-ALPR, plate-restoration GAN papers, attention-based plate super-resolution) is essentially absent. Section 2.2 cites two surveys and one YOLO paper. Also missing: any urban-sensing literature on CCTV geometry priors (Zhang et al., camera pose estimation in smart cities), Street-View / dashcam privacy work, and recent multi-modal urban imaging surveys. For a CEUS submission the urban-systems coverage is thin.

9. **Figure/table issues after revision.** Fig. 5 is described in Section 5.2 as visualising F (right) and AUC (left), yet it is a slope-graph of dataset-shift sensitivity; Section 5.2 then immediately pivots to "OCR accuracy heatmaps" that no longer appear in the paper (the comment at line 1023 says "removed Fig 6 recoverability boundary schematic"). The OCR heatmap and recoverability boundary schematic were evidently dropped during revision but the prose referencing them was not fully rewritten, leaving a dangling figure reference and a section that describes visualisations the reader cannot see. Fig. 8 (qualitative comparison) is useful but the caption does not specify which three angle regimes are shown. Table 4's "best F on DS-E" for Restormer is 0.209, which is not the lowest F on DS-E (U-Net Conditional has 0.115); the bolding is inconsistent with the caption rule ("best AUC per dataset in bold") and will confuse readers.

10. **Contribution claim about PSNR as an OCR proxy is overstated.** R^2 = 0.99 is reported only when points are aggregated across the angle grid, where PSNR spans roughly 12-35 dB. At a fixed angle or fixed plate, the PSNR-OCR relationship is far noisier. Practitioners selecting checkpoints during training do not see the full angular spread; they see one validation set. The paper should clarify whether the "training proxy" recommendation survives when the validation pool is narrower.

## 5. Specific comments

- **Abstract, lines ~399-404.** "Recovery fails beyond approximately 80 degrees in both axes simultaneously, with alpha rotations consistently harder than beta." This is stated as a physical fact but is actually specific to this particular distortion pipeline (homography + fixed focal length + fixed distance). Qualify.
- **Section 3, Eq. 1.** The forward model has distortion D applied after projection Pi, yet Section 4.1 describes a five-stage pipeline that ends with "de-warp and resize". The de-warp step is not in Eq. 1. Clarify whether the network input is the pre-dewarp or post-dewarp image; this materially affects how readers interpret the angular boundary.
- **Section 3, Eqs. 3-4.** beta_max(alpha) is defined as the max beta in [0,89] with r_T = 1, but if r_T(alpha, 0) = 0 the definition returns 0, which conflates "boundary at 0" with "never recoverable". Handle this edge case.
- **Section 4.1.** "Two datasets are constructed (Table 1)" but Section 5.1 reports results for "both datasets" while experiment_data.md describes three. Reconcile.
- **Section 4.4.** Tesseract's "contrast-normalised, upscaled 2x, binarised" pre-processing is itself doing restoration work. The reported OCR accuracies mix network-restoration quality with Tesseract-pipeline quality. This should be acknowledged as a limitation.
- **Section 5.1.** The sentence "absolute PSNR drops by ~3-4 dB on the Extreme dataset" is correct against Table 3 but experiment_data.md Table 5 describes a Dataset C in exactly this role with slightly different numbers; check which is authoritative.
- **Section 5.2.** Opens by referencing heatmaps that are no longer present. Rewrite.
- **Section 5.3.** "Across all ten model-dataset pairs the per-model boundaries cluster tightly (see Table 4)". With 5 models x 2 datasets = 10, fine, but experiment_data.md has 15 (5x3). Reconcile.
- **Section 5.4.** Restormer DS-E F = 0.209 is the second-worst among discriminative models, not the best; the prose "its global attention mechanism is robust to the choice of angle distribution" does not follow from this number.
- **Section 6.1.** The claim "software-only restoration pipelines are sufficient for LPR without any hardware upgrade" is far too strong given synthetic-only evaluation.
- **Section 6.3.** Refers to a 20,000-sample dataset not reported in Table 1.
- **Fig. 1.** Caption claims AI-enabled restoration "recovers readable license plates from severely oblique imagery that conventional LPR systems cannot process", which is the paper's conclusion, not an input premise. Rewrite to be descriptive rather than assertive.
- **Fig. 2.** The Sobol-density surfaces are visually handsome but hard to read for the purpose they are meant to serve (comparing the two PDFs). A 2-D colormap with matched color scale would be more informative.
- **References.** Missing Laroca et al. RodoSol-ALPR (2022), Silva & Jung WPOD-NET, Lin et al. plate super-resolution, anything on smart-city CCTV geometry estimation.

## 6. Recommendation

**Major Revision.** The core experiment is sound and the framing question is timely, but three fundamental issues (synthetic-only evaluation presented as a deployment guarantee; internal inconsistency between the paper and the experimental record; a thin urban-systems contribution for a CEUS submission) all need to be addressed before acceptance. The core benchmark is worth publishing once reconciled and properly bounded; it is not worth publishing in its current form.

## 7. Required revisions before acceptance

1. **Reconcile the paper with the experimental record.** Either restore the missing dataset(s) and update every table/figure/claim, or remove all residual references to datasets and sample counts not reported in Table 1. No dangling figure/section references.
2. **Add a real-data sanity check.** At minimum, run the best discriminative model on a publicly available oblique-view plate dataset (UFPR-ALPR, RodoSol-ALPR, CCPD) and report whether the angular boundary is consistent. A single-figure validation is sufficient to support the synthetic finding; without it the 93.4% number cannot be claimed.
3. **Multiple seeds and confidence intervals.** At least 3 seeds per model x dataset cell; 95% bootstrap CIs on AUC and F; significance test for inter-model rankings. If any ranking flips inside the CI, revise the claims accordingly.
4. **Reframe boundary-AUC and F as adapted rather than novel metrics.** Cite relevant prior work on 2-D recoverability envelopes and erosion-type reliability measures. Soften "novel" to "adapted".
5. **Strengthen the urban-systems contribution.** Provide a quantitative mapping from each named sensor class (ATM, body-worn, dashcam, pole CCTV) to a plausible (alpha, beta) prior distribution, using either published camera-geometry data or a small field survey; overlay those priors on the recoverability heatmap so the paper answers the urban-planning question directly. Without this, the CEUS fit remains weak.
6. **Release code, trained weights, Sobol seeds, and the evaluation grid data.** A benchmark paper without artefacts cannot be accepted at a reputable journal.
7. **Replace or supplement Tesseract v4 with a contemporary plate-OCR model.** Report sensitivity of AUC, F, and the 93.4% figure to OCR choice.
8. **Fix dangling figure and section references from the recent revision.** Rewrite Section 5.2 so it describes only figures that are present; reconcile Fig. 5 caption and prose; correct the Table 4 bolding rule; name the angle regimes shown in Fig. 8.
9. **Broaden the related-work coverage** to include oblique-LPR, plate super-resolution, and urban-sensor-geometry literature; tighten the opportunistic-sensing framing so its invocation is substantive rather than decorative.
10. **Soften Abstract and Conclusion language.** Explicitly scope the 93.4% figure and the "software-only is sufficient" statement to the synthetic Israeli-plate, 256x64, Tesseract setting. State the caveat the first time the number is introduced, not only in Section 6.4.
